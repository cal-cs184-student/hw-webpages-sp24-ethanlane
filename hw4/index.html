Overview

In this project, we explain the intersection of rendering with physical simulation. To simulate the way that cloth behaves, we model it as a network of interconnected springs, each spring acting to enforce a realistic approximation of how cloth behaves. By considering the fabric as comprised of point masses subject to these spring constraints, we get a realistic simulation because fabric is essentially finely woven slightly elastic threads. There is a great variety of the types of fabric in the world, and for this reason, we set the properties as variable, allowing for our program to emulate fabrics that are light or dense, tight or loose, etc. We also add constraints to prevent collisions both with the fabric itself, as well as with primitives 3d meshes such as spheres and planes. This allows us to see how the cloth would behave not just in isolation, but also in the context of dropping into a scene and finally settling into a still position. Additionally, we go beyond a simple rendering that just implies white lines representing the springs and dots to represent the point masses–we apply a variety of shaders which can give the cloth texture, shininess, or just a realistic matte rendering.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration
Experiment with some the parameters in the simulation. To do so, pause the simulation at the start with P, modify the values of interest, and then resume by pressing P again. You can also restart the simulation at any time from the cloth's starting position by pressing R.
Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

The spring constant ks is directly proportional to the amount of force applied by the spring at a certain extension length. For this reason, with a high ks, the fabric is much tighter, giving the appearance of a thicker and less flexible fabric, whereas with a low ks, there is more apparent malleability, and the fabric appears to deform much more freely.

What about for density?

A higher density essentially acts the same as decreasing the spring constant. This is because with a greater density, the forces from the springs do not carry the same amount of influence since the acceleration is proportional to the mass of the point. At the same time, the denser material is heavier at each point mass, and thus has more inertia in motion, so it appears to move more sluggishly.

What about for damping?

Because the fabric is held together by spring forces, it is prone to oscillations. With a low amount of damping, this oscillation is more apparent, although when the damping is increased, this oscillation decays faster. This has the effect of making the cloth settle faster when the damping constant is higher.

For each of the above, observe any noticeable differences in the cloth compared to the default parameters and show us some screenshots of those interesting differences and describe when they occur.

When ks is higher, the cloth is tighter and has less wrinkles. When ks is lower, the cloth is looser and has more apparent wrinkles. When the damping is higher, the cloth settles into a balanced position slowly and steadily. When the damping is lower, the cloth overshoots the equilibrium position and oscillates back and forth, taking longer to finally stabilize.

Part 5: Cloth Sim
Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

The vertex aspect of the shader causes a movement in the location of the vertex, and this alone causes the perception of texture. On the other hand the fragment shader changes the way in which light is reflected from a vertex, but does not alter its position. In this way, the fragment shader is more like a specification of the material’s light reflecting or emitting properties, while the vertex shader gives the ability for a more simple mesh (e.g. a flat cloth) to be rendered using a more subtle and complex geometry (such as shifting each vertex by a certain height map offset).

Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

Blinn-Phong shading combines the ambient background light in the environment, with the diffuse reflection, as well as a specular reflectance. The first component gives a general amount of backlighting to the entire object, and ensures that none of the object is obscured in total darkness. The aspect of diffuse reflection gives a smooth shading that gives the object three dimensional pop, while the specular reflectance gives the shiny quality (it is the brightness from the direct reflection of the light source to the camera bouncing off the object head on. Compared to diffuse shading, Blinn-Phong gives a much more realistic and shiny appearance to the objects.

Show a screenshot of bump mapping on the cloth and on the sphere. Show a screenshot of displacement mapping on the sphere. Use the same texture for both renders. You can either provide your own texture or use one of the ones in the textures directory, BUT choose one that's not the default texture_2.png. Compare the two approaches and resulting renders in your own words. Compare how your the two shaders react to the sphere by changing the sphere mesh's coarseness by using -o 16 -a 16 and then -o 128 -a 128.

Bump shading gives a similar effect to displacement shading, and in fact, they share essentially the same implementation, except that in displacement shading, there is also vertex shifting that lift the texture to another level. While bump shading works better in course meshes, in my aesthetic opinion, displacement shading does not work as well in a course mesh. This is because the height map, which is at a higher frequency than the mesh by a considerable degree, fails to accurately capture the texture. The mesh needs to be fine enough for the vertex displacements to actually capture something meaningful about that texture, otherwise it looks misshaped, and less aesthetically pleasing.

Show a screenshot of your mirror shader on the cloth and on the sphere.
Explain what you did in your custom shader, if you made one.

In this custom shader, we combined Phong shading with mirror shading, and this gives the effect of a more realistic shiny surface, with a more matte appearance. We also added in some vertex displacement to further boost the surface by giving it the appearance of having some chromatic abrasions that are more lifelike. While the original mirror shading looks nice, this approach boosts the realism since in reality, even a shiny surface also has components of diffuse lighting and physical imperfections in the surface–more like a chrome ball.
